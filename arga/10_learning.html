The AI Tutor

Using an LLM to Learn (and Prove You've Learned) a New Topic

Introduction & Goals

Today's exercise is to learn a new, unfamiliar paper or research area using an LLM as your personal tutor.

The Core Challenge: Passive vs. Active Learning

It's easy to read an AI's explanation and think you understand it. This is passive learning and is often shallow. We want active learning, where you can prove you've retained and can apply the knowledge.

To fight this, we will use a "Test-First" Approach. You will use an LLM to generate a "final exam" for your chosen topic *before* you start learning it. You will then study the topic, and finally, take the exam to see what you *actually* learned.

Part 1: The "Pre-Test" - Define Your Goal

First, you must choose your topic and generate your test. You should not study the topic or read the paper before you do this.

Task 1: Select Your Unfamiliar Topic

Choose a paper or a niche research area that you are unfamiliar with or want to learn about, even if you know the surrounding literature. This should be a topic you haven't had time to dive into yet.

If you're stuck, ask a neighbor to recommend a foundational paper from their field. This is a great way to get a topic you aren't already biased about.

Task 2: Generate Your "Final Exam" (Before You Study)

This is the most important step. Before you ask the AI to explain *anything*, ask it to create the test you'll take at the end.

Example Prompt
Act as an expert professor in [Field of the Paper]. I have attached a research paper titled "[Paper Title]". Please generate a comprehensive 10-question test (a mix of 5 high-level conceptual questions and 5 low-level detail-oriented questions) that a student who *fully understands* this paper and its context should be able to answer. Do NOT provide the answers. Just list the 10 questions.

Save Your Test!

Create a new Google Doc. Copy and paste these 10 questions into it and save the document in the class folder below. This is your "exam."

Part 2: The "Study Session" - Interactive Learning

Now you will use the LLM as your tutor to learn the material, going from the 30,000-foot view down to the details. This is an open-ended process. Be curious and ask follow-up questions.

Task 1: High-Level Scaffolding (The "Big Picture")

Use prompts to understand the context, importance, and main ideas.

Example Prompts
"Explain this paper to me like I'm a graduate student in a completely different field. What is the core problem it's trying to solve?" "Why is this work important? What specific work does this paper build on or respond to?" "What is the main 'contribution' or 'finding' of this paper?"

Task 2: Low-Level Deep Dive (The "Nitty-Gritty")

This is where you tackle the parts you find confusing. Be specific.

Example Prompts
"Let's walk through Section 3.1. Can you explain Equation (2) in simpler terms? What does each variable represent?" "I don't understand the logic of the proof for Theorem 1. Can you explain the key steps?" "In the experiment section, what is the 'baseline model' they are comparing against, and why did they choose it?" "Generate pseudo-code for the algorithm described in Figure 4."

Part 3: The "Self-Assessment" - Check Your Confidence

You have finished your study session. Before you look at the test questions again, you must perform a self-assessment.

Task: Rate Your Understanding

On a scale of 1-10, how well do you feel you understand the material right now? 1 means "not at all," 10 means "I could explain this to a professor."

Write this number down in your Google Doc, *before* you take the test.

Warning: The Illusion of Understanding

A growing body of evidence suggests that the fluency of LLM explanations can be deceptive. Because the answer is so clear and confident, it can make you *feel* like you've learned the material, even if you haven't truly internalized it.

This "illusion of understanding" is exactly what we are testing for. Be honest with your self-assessment. We will compare this number to your actual test score.

Part 4: The "Final Exam" - Verifying Your Knowledge

This is the moment of truth. You will now test what you *actually* learned.

Task 1: Take the Test (No AI Help)

Open your Google Doc with the 10 questions from Part 1. Close any browser tabs with the AI tutor.

Without asking the LLM for help, write out your answers to all 10 questions from memory. Do your best to be thorough.

Task 2: Get Graded by the AI

Start a new, fresh LLM session (to be safe). Provide the AI with the original paper, your questions, and your answers.

Example Prompt
Act as a strict but fair grader. I have attached the original research paper. I also have a list of 10 questions about this paper, along with my answers. Your task is to grade my answers. For each question, tell me if my answer is: - Correct - Partially Correct - Incorrect After each grade, you MUST provide a 1-2 sentence justification for your grade, explaining *why* my answer was right or wrong. [Attach the original paper] --- Here are the questions and my answers: [Paste the 10 Questions + Your 10 Answers here]

Reflection

For Class Discussion

Read the AI's grades and justifications. Think about the following questions:

  • What was your self-assessment score (1-10)? What was your actual score on the test (e.g., 7/10)? How big was the gap between your perceived and actual understanding?
  • What kinds of questions did you get right (e.g., high-level) vs. wrong (e.g., low-level/math)?
  • Was the AI's grading fair? Did it correctly identify your misunderstandings?
  • What was the most effective prompt you used during your "Study Session" (Part 2) that led to a "correct" answer on the test?
  • What are the biggest risks or blind spots of learning a new topic *only* from an LLM? (e.g., Did it have a specific bias? Did it fail to explain something?)
  • How will this exercise change the way you use LLMs for learning in the future?
